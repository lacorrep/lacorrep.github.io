Modeling inflatable panels

Nonlinear plate theory

Erreur avec la balise canvas

A plate : basically a doormat

Our goal is to obtain 2D equations that describe our 3D system. Since the geometry of the panel is closer to a 2D body than a 3D one, it seems reasonable to focus our efforts on the two in-plane dimensions.

A plate is modeled as a plane onto which a "field" of fibers is attached. Each point of the mid-surface is pierced by a straight line, called the normal fiber. This name is misleading: the fiber is only normal in the initial configuration. We work with the Mindlin–Reissner kinematics assumption: \[ \vec a_3 = \tens R \cdot \vec A_3 \] with $$\tens R$$ a rotation tensor and $$\vec A_3$$ the initial normal fiber. This means that the fibers behave as rigid bodies; they may rotate but not stretch.

This yields the following displacement field: \[\vec U ( \vec Q_0,t ) = \vec U (\vec P_0, t) + Z (\tens R - \tens I) \vec A_3\]

Follower forces

Inflatable structures acquire their rigidity from internal pressure.

\[ \vec t = p \vec n \]

The virtual power of the internal pressure forces is expressed as:

\[ \mathscr{P}^*_{\text{pressure}} = \int_{\Omega} p \vec n \cdot \vec U^*(\vec Q_0,t) \mathrm d \Omega \]

$$\vec n$$ must be calculated in the deformed configuration and written mathematically as a function of quantities defined on the mid-surface. It must also be independent of $$Z$$.
